# Sploink x Seoro — Partnership Proposal
**Draft | March 12, 2026 | Timothy Nguyen + Benjamin Moon**

---

## The Premise

Two companies. Two founders. One shared vision.

Sploink owns the infrastructure — the telemetry, the routing, the intelligence layer that makes agents measurable and rankable. Seoro owns the surface — the ambient AI experience that sits closest to the user.

Neither is complete without the other. Sploink without a consumer surface is infrastructure with no face. Seoro without a routing and measurement layer is a beautiful app with no brain. Together, the stack is end-to-end: from what the user sees, to how requests are routed, to how agent performance is measured and improved over time.

This proposal is about formalizing that relationship — on equal footing, with full independence, and with protections that make sure neither company can ever be used as a weapon against the other.

---

## What We're Each Building

**Sploink (Timothy + team)**
The intelligence layer. Low-level agent telemetry, behavioral measurement, convergence and thrash detection, agent routing via Salaxia, and eventually an agent skills marketplace. This is the infrastructure that any serious ambient AI product needs underneath it. The moat is the data — every agent execution makes the ranking smarter.

**Seoro (Ben + team)**
The perception layer. The ambient AI consumer surface that sits closest to the user — reading context, understanding intent, and delivering experiences that feel seamless. The moat is the UX and the distribution — getting into users' daily workflows in a way that infrastructure alone never can.

---

## Why We're Better Together

### 1. The stack is vertically complete
Seoro + Sploink covers the full AI agent stack: perception → routing → measurement. No competitor has all three layers. Every YC company in this space owns one. We own the whole thing — if we work together.

### 2. Seoro gets a brain it can't build alone
Routing intelligence and behavioral telemetry takes years to build well. The reputation graph, the skills ranking, the convergence signals — Seoro gets all of that as infrastructure, so Ben can focus on what he's best at: the consumer product and distribution.

### 3. Sploink gets a consumer surface and real user data
B2C ambient AI users generate behavioral data that enterprise agents don't. Seoro gives Sploink access to consumer-level execution traces — a data source that compounds the ranking system's intelligence over time. Without a consumer surface, Sploink's dataset skews enterprise-only.

### 4. Go-to-market is stronger together
A complete vertical stack is a better story for investors, press, and enterprise customers than two separate companies pitching adjacent products. "We own the full AI agent stack from perception to measurement" is a fundable thesis. Two separate pitches are harder.

### 5. Combined, we're harder to replicate
Infrastructure without distribution is a commodity. Distribution without infrastructure is fragile. Together, the switching cost for any customer or user is extremely high — they'd have to replace the entire stack, not just one layer.

## The Structure

### Full ownership separation
Timothy fully owns Sploink and Salaxia. Ben fully owns Seoro. No shared equity at the operating company level. Each founder controls their own company, their own cap table, their own team, and their own roadmap.

### Cross-equity for alignment
To keep incentives pointed in the same direction:

- Seoro issues Sploink **10-15% equity** — reflecting Seoro's dependency on Sploink's infrastructure
- Sploink issues Seoro **3-5% equity** — reflecting Seoro as a key distribution channel and data source

Both stakes vest over 2 years with a 12-month cliff. Equity is earned when the product ships, not when the deal is signed. Neither party has board seats in the other company.

The asymmetry is intentional and honest. Seoro is more dependent on Sploink's stack than Sploink is on Seoro's surface. That dependency has a price. In exchange, Seoro gets world-class infrastructure it couldn't build alone.

### The Holdco — governance only, no equity
A lightweight Holdco binds the two companies through rules, not ownership. No equity lives at the Holdco level. It exists solely to enforce the partnership terms and prevent either company from acting against the other's interests.

**The rules the Holdco enforces:**

1. **Data sharing** — Sploink shares routing and telemetry insights with Seoro. Seoro shares consumer behavioral data with Sploink. Neither can cut the other off.

2. **API access** — Seoro gets preferred, guaranteed access to Sploink's routing and telemetry APIs. Sploink cannot deprioritize or price-gouge Seoro in favor of other customers.

3. **Non-compete** — Sploink does not build a consumer ambient AI surface that competes with Seoro. Seoro does not build a routing or telemetry layer that competes with Sploink.

4. **Right of first conversation** — If either company receives an acquisition offer, the other is notified first. Neither is obligated to block the deal, but both are entitled to the conversation.

5. **Performance-weighted governance** — As the partnership matures, governance decisions affecting both companies are weighted by revenue contribution. The company generating more value earns more say in joint decisions. No artificial equality — the market decides who leads.

6. **Mutual protection** — Neither company can take an action that materially harms the other without 30 days notice and a good-faith attempt to resolve. This includes pivots, pricing changes, or partnerships with direct competitors of the other.

### Commercial API agreement (separate)
Independent of equity, Seoro pays Sploink commercially for API usage — per execution, per routing call, per telemetry event. Preferred rates as a partner, but not free. This keeps the relationship honest: Seoro is a customer of Sploink's infrastructure, and that has real value.

## Protections for Both Sides

**How Sploink can't screw Seoro:**
- Cannot cut off API access without 90-day notice
- Cannot raise Seoro's API pricing above a defined cap without renegotiation
- Cannot build a competing consumer product while the partnership is active
- Seoro's 3-5% stake in Sploink means Ben participates in Sploink's upside regardless

**How Seoro can't screw Sploink:**
- Cannot build or license a competing routing/telemetry layer while the partnership is active
- Cannot take an acquisition that transfers Sploink's API access to a competitor without consent
- Sploink's 10-15% stake in Seoro means Timothy participates in Seoro's upside regardless

**How we exit cleanly if it doesn't work:**
Either party can exit the partnership with 6 months notice. Cross-equity stakes are retained — you keep what you've vested. API access continues at standard commercial rates. The non-compete ends 12 months after exit. No scorched earth.

**What happens when one company's failure starts hurting the other:**
This is the hardest scenario and needs explicit rules.

*Trigger conditions* — either of the following activates the provisions below:
- One company misses 3 consecutive shared milestones with no credible recovery plan
- One company's instability (financial, legal, team) materially degrades the integration (uptime, API reliability, data quality)
- One company pivots away from the shared stack without 60 days notice

*What happens when triggered:*

1. **30-day notice period.** The affected company notifies the other in writing. This starts a mandatory good-faith resolution window — both parties must attempt to fix the problem before any exit is triggered.

2. **60-day build protection.** Neither party can exit the partnership until at least 6 months after Phase 1's shared milestone is hit (first real user session through the full stack). You don't get to leave before you've given the partnership a real chance. This protects both founders from cold feet killing something that just needed more time.

3. **Structured wind-down if unresolved.** If the 30-day window doesn't resolve the issue, the affected company can trigger a structured separation: API access steps down over 90 days (not cut off overnight), cross-equity stakes freeze at current vesting, non-compete terms remain in effect for 12 months post-separation.

4. **The failing company cannot hold the other hostage.** If Seoro is failing, it cannot block Sploink from finding other consumer partners. If Sploink is failing, Seoro retains the right to build or license an alternative routing layer. Failure on one side does not trap the other.

5. **Equity is not clawed back.** Whatever has vested stays vested. Failure is not punished retroactively — only future vesting stops.

## The Long-Term Vision

**B2B first (now — 2026):**
Sploink builds the infrastructure moat with enterprise customers. Seoro builds the consumer product. Both compound independently.

**B2B + B2C together (2027):**
Seoro becomes the consumer face of Sploink's agent stack. Enterprise customers use Sploink directly. Consumer users experience it through Seoro. The data from both compounds the ranking and routing intelligence.

**Agent OS (2027-2028):**
The full ambient AI vision. Perception (Seoro) → routing (Salaxia) → measurement (Sploink) → OS-level agent infrastructure. A vertically integrated stack that no single-layer competitor can replicate.

This only exists if both companies are strong. A weak Seoro means no consumer surface. A weak Sploink means no intelligence layer. We need each other to be successful for the long-term vision to be real.

---

## What This Is Not

- Not a merger. Two companies, two founders, full independence.
- Not a 50/50 split. Governance is weighted by contribution over time.
- Not a handshake deal. The Holdco terms are legally binding on both parties.
- Not permanent if it doesn't work. Clean exit terms exist for a reason.

## How We Build Together

This is the practical answer to "okay we agreed — now what do we actually do?"

### Phase 1: Foundation (Now — April 2026)

**Sploink's job:**
- Ship the CLI wrapper — wraps any agent session, captures low-level telemetry (file edits, shell commands, git state, build/test output)
- Lock the canonical event schema — the data contract everything downstream depends on
- Expose POST /execute and POST /telemetry-ingest as stable APIs
- Ship Salaxia's HTTP server (POST /intent) so Seoro has something to call

**Seoro's job:**
- Define the intent format — what does Seoro send to Salaxia when a user makes a request?
- Build against Sploink's API in staging — don't wait for production
- Ship at least one end-to-end flow: user request → Seoro captures context → Salaxia routes → Sploink executes → result back to Seoro

**The integration point:**
```
Seoro captures user context
  → POST /intent to Salaxia (task_id, context, user signal)
  → Salaxia routes to best agent
  → Sploink executes + captures telemetry
  → Telemetry feeds back to Salaxia for ranking
  → Result returns to Seoro for display
```

This loop is the product. Everything else is secondary until this works end-to-end.

**Shared milestone:** One real user session flowing through the full stack by end of April.

---

### Phase 2: Signal + Intelligence (May — July 2026)

**Sploink's job:**
- Thrash detection live — flag sessions where agents are looping without progress
- Convergence scoring — real-time signal on whether a session is making progress
- Dashboard v1 — CLI-adjacent visualization showing convergence curve, thrash alerts, token/time costs
- Skills ranking — start scoring individual skills (not just full agents) based on execution traces

**Seoro's job:**
- Surface Sploink's signals inside the Seoro UI — show users when their agent is thrashing, suggest intervention
- Goal attribution — map user intent (the original prompt) to sub-goals, feed that structure to Sploink so telemetry is tagged by sub-goal not just session
- First 100 users — Seoro owns consumer distribution, start getting real users through the full stack

**The integration point:**
Seoro becomes the user-facing layer for Sploink's intelligence. When Sploink detects thrash, Seoro surfaces the alert. When Sploink scores a skill highly, Seoro recommends it. The intelligence is Sploink's. The experience is Seoro's.

**Shared milestone:** 100 users running agent sessions through the full stack. Thrash detection catching real loops in production.

---

### Phase 3: Marketplace + Scale (Q3 2026 onwards)

**Sploink's job:**
- Agent and skills marketplace — ranking, discovery, reputation graph live
- Agent self-registration API — any developer can submit an agent, it gets scored automatically
- pgvector migration — replace file-based storage with real vector search for intent matching at scale
- eBPF exploration — begin kernel-level telemetry research for Phase 4

**Seoro's job:**
- Consumer distribution at scale — Seoro is the front door to the marketplace for consumer users
- Ambient AI features — context fusion, proactive nudges, longitudinal user memory
- Enterprise conversations — large teams using Seoro as the ambient AI layer on top of Sploink's enterprise APIs

**The integration point:**
The marketplace lives in Sploink. Seoro is the consumer storefront. Enterprise customers access it directly via API. Both channels feed the reputation graph, which makes routing smarter, which makes both products better.

**Shared milestone:** 10 agents in the marketplace with real execution histories. Sploink routing measurably improving agent outcomes over time.

### How Decisions Get Made

| Type of decision | Who decides |
|---|---|
| Sploink product roadmap | Timothy |
| Seoro product roadmap | Ben |
| API contract changes | Joint — 30 days notice required |
| Pricing changes affecting the other | Joint — renegotiation required |
| New integrations that affect both stacks | Joint — align before building |
| Each company's hiring, equity, fundraising | Fully independent |

No committee. No approval chains. Each founder runs their company. The only joint decisions are the ones that touch the integration layer — because that's where we can actually hurt each other.
